I think that the description of the setting in the second chapter is telling of the plight of the people who live there. It falters in disrepair and neglect. K's passion betrays him. His insistence that "none of this concerns me" (Chapter 2) is naturally contrasted by striking "the desk with his fist" which highlights his lack of temperance. Perhaps it is possible that people back then spoke like that, but his diatribes on the injustice of this arrest and the incompetency of this organisation that has warranted such an arrest that stretch excessively in such a place as a courtroom, no matter how informal it may appear, shows a level of disrespect and grandiousity. It is natural for a person to feel --- perhaps rightfully so --- hurt and insulted by such an event, but his response drips with hatred and reveals his bitterness. He also postures to the crowd instead of sharing a dialogue with the judge, referring to the audience, "gentlemen", asking "how are we to avoid those in office becoming ... corrupt". K appears unable to empathise with fellow humans.
